<p><b>Pocahontas</b> (born <b>Matoaka</b>, known as <b>Amonute</b>, and later known as <b>Rebecca Rolfe</b>, <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 1595&#160;– March 1617) was a <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Indian" title="Virginia Indian" class="mw-redirect">Virginia Indian</a><sup id="cite_ref-VIwriting_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VIwriting-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Poca_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Poca-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> notable for her association with the colonial settlement at <a href="/wiki/Jamestown,_Virginia" title="Jamestown, Virginia">Jamestown, Virginia</a>. Pocahontas was the daughter of <a href="/wiki/Chief_Powhatan" title="Chief Powhatan">Powhatan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Paramount_chief" title="Paramount chief">paramount chief</a><sup id="cite_ref-VIwriting_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VIwriting-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> of a network of tributary tribal nations in the <a href="/wiki/Tsenacommacah" title="Tsenacommacah">Tsenacommacah</a>, encompassing the <a href="/wiki/Tidewater_region" title="Tidewater region">Tidewater region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>. In a well-known historical anecdote, she is said to have saved the life of an Indian captive, Englishman <a href="/wiki/John_Smith_(explorer)" title="John Smith (explorer)">John Smith</a>, in 1607 by placing her head upon his own when her father raised his war club to execute him. The general consensus of historians is that this story, as told by Smith, is untrue.<sup id="cite_ref-NMAI_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NMAI-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Pocahontas was captured by the English during Anglo-Indian hostilities in 1613, and held for ransom. During her captivity, she converted to <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> and took the name Rebecca. When the opportunity arose for her to return to her people, she chose to remain with the English. In April 1614, she married tobacco planter <a href="/wiki/John_Rolfe" title="John Rolfe">John Rolfe</a>, and in January 1615, bore their son, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Rolfe" title="Thomas Rolfe">Thomas Rolfe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stebbins_2010_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stebbins_2010-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1616, the Rolfes traveled to London. Pocahontas was presented to English society as an example of the <a href="/wiki/Noble_savage" title="Noble savage">"civilized savage"</a> in hopes of stimulating investment in the Jamestown settlement. She became something of a celebrity, was elegantly fêted, and attended a <a href="/wiki/Masque" title="Masque">masque</a> at <a href="/wiki/Whitehall_Palace" title="Whitehall Palace" class="mw-redirect">Whitehall Palace</a>. In 1617, the Rolfes set sail for Virginia, but Pocahontas died at <a href="/wiki/Gravesend,_Kent" title="Gravesend, Kent" class="mw-redirect">Gravesend</a> of unknown causes. She was buried in a church in Gravesend in the United Kingdom, but the exact location of her grave is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-Stebbins_2010_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stebbins_2010-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Numerous places, landmarks, and products in the United States have been named after Pocahontas. Her story has been romanticized over the years, and she is a subject of art, literature, and film. Her descendants, through her son Thomas, include members of the <a href="/wiki/First_Families_of_Virginia" title="First Families of Virginia">First Families of Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/First_Lady_of_the_United_States" title="First Lady of the United States">First Lady</a> <a href="/wiki/Edith_Wilson" title="Edith Wilson">Edith Wilson</a>, American Western actor <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Strange" title="Glenn Strange">Glenn Strange</a>, and astronomer <a href="/wiki/Percival_Lowell" title="Percival Lowell">Percival Lowell</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pocahontas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pocahontas's birth year is unknown, but some historians estimate it to have been around 1596.<sup id="cite_ref-Stebbins_2010_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stebbins_2010-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> In <i>A True Relation of Virginia</i> (1608), Smith described the Pocahontas he met in the spring of 1608 as being "a child of tenne years old".<sup id="cite_ref-Smith93_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith93-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> In a letter written in 1616, he again described her as she was in 1608, but this time she had grown slightly to "a child of twelve or thirteen years of age".<sup id="cite_ref-Letter_to_Queen_Anne_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Letter_to_Queen_Anne-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Pocahontas was the daughter of <a href="/wiki/Chief_Powhatan" title="Chief Powhatan">Powhatan</a>, the paramount chief of <a href="/wiki/Tsenacommacah" title="Tsenacommacah">Tsenacommacah</a>, an alliance of about thirty <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Algonquian_languages" title="Eastern Algonquian languages">Algonquian-speaking</a> groups and petty chiefdoms in <a href="/wiki/Tidewater_region" title="Tidewater region">Tidewater, Virginia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Her mother, whose name and specific group of origin are unknown, was one of dozens of wives taken by Powhatan; each wife gave him a single child and then was sent back to her village to be supported by the paramount chief until she found another husband.<sup id="cite_ref-RountreeHelen_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RountreeHelen-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Pocahontas's childhood was probably little different from that of most girls who lived in Tsenacommacah. She learned how to perform what was considered to be women's work, which included foraging for food and firewood, farming, and searching for the plant materials used in building thatched houses.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> As she grew older, she helped other members of Powhatan's household with preparations for large feasts.<sup id="cite_ref-RountreeHelen_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RountreeHelen-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Serving feasts, such as the one presented to John Smith after his capture, was a regular obligation of the <i>Mamanatowick</i>, or paramount chief.<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_Virginia_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia_Virginia-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Names">Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pocahontas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the time Pocahontas was born, it was common for Powhatan Native Americans to be given several personal names, have more than one name at the same time, have secret names that only a select few knew, and to change their names on important occasions. Bestowed at different times, the names carried different meanings and might be used in different contexts.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Pocahontas was no different. Early in her life she was given a secret name, Matoaka, but later she was also known as Amonute. Matoaka means "Bright Stream Between the Hills"; Amonute cannot be translated.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The name Pocahontas was a childhood nickname that probably referred to her frolicsome nature; according to the colonist <a href="/wiki/William_Strachey" title="William Strachey">William Strachey</a>, it meant "little wanton",<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> some interpret the meaning as "playful one".<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_Virginia_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia_Virginia-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The 18th-century historian <a href="/wiki/William_Stith" title="William Stith">William Stith</a> claimed that "her real name, it seems, was originally Matoax, which the Indians carefully concealed from the English and changed it to Pocahontas, out of a superstitious fear, lest they, by the knowledge of her true name, should be enabled to do her some hurt."<sup id="cite_ref-Stith1_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stith1-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> According to the anthropologist Helen C. Rountree, Pocahontas "revealed [her secret name] to the English only after she had taken another religious—baptismal—name, Rebecca".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Pocahontas's Christian name, Rebecca, may have been a symbolic gesture to <a href="/wiki/Rebecca" title="Rebecca">Rebecca</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Book of Genesis</a> who, as the mother of <a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">Jacob</a> and <a href="/wiki/Esau" title="Esau">Esau</a>, was the mother of two "nations", or distinct peoples. Pocahontas, as a Powhatan marrying an Englishman, may have been seen by herself and by her contemporaries as being also, potentially, the mother of two nations.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Title_and_status">Title and status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pocahontas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Title and status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pocahontas has been considered in popular culture to be a <a href="/wiki/Princess" title="Princess">princess</a>. In 1841, William Watson Waldron of <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Dublin" title="Trinity College, Dublin">Trinity College, Dublin</a>, in Ireland, published <i>Pocahontas, American Princess: and Other Poems</i>, calling Pocahontas "the beloved and only surviving daughter of the king".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Indeed, Pocahontas was a favorite of her father's—his "delight and darling", according to the colonist <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Hamor" title="Ralph Hamor">Captain Ralph Hamor</a><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup>—but she was not in line to inherit a position as a <i><a href="/wiki/Weroance" title="Weroance">weroance</a></i>, subchief, or <i>mamanatowick</i> (paramount chief). Instead, Powhatan's brothers, sisters, and his sisters' children all stood in line to succeed him.<sup id="cite_ref-RountreeHelen_b_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RountreeHelen_b-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> In his <i>A Map of Virginia</i> John Smith explained how matrilineal inheritance worked among the Powhatans:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>His [Powhatan's] kingdome descendeth not to his sonnes nor children: but first to his brethren, whereof he hath three namely Opitchapan, <a href="/wiki/Opchanacanough" title="Opchanacanough">Opechanncanough</a>, and Catataugh; and after their decease to his sisters. First to the eldest sister, then to the rest: and after them to the heires male and female of the eldest sister; but never to the heires of the males.</p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mother">Mother</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pocahontas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mother">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The status of Nonoma Winanuske Matatiske, Pocahontas's mother, would have been lowly. In his <i>Relation of Virginia</i> (1609), the colonist <a href="/wiki/Henry_Spelman_of_Jamestown" title="Henry Spelman of Jamestown">Henry Spelman</a>, who had lived among the Powhatan serving as an interpreter, noted Chief Powhatan's many wives. Each wife gave the paramount chief one child, after which she not only resumed her status as a commoner but was also sent back where she had come from.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> However, some historians even theorize her mother died during childbirth.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interactions_with_the_English">Interactions with the English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pocahontas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Interactions with the English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="John_Smith">John Smith</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pocahontas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: John Smith">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pocahontas-saves-Smith-NE-Chromo-1870.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Pocahontas-saves-Smith-NE-Chromo-1870.jpeg/290px-Pocahontas-saves-Smith-NE-Chromo-1870.jpeg" width="290" height="217" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Pocahontas-saves-Smith-NE-Chromo-1870.jpeg/435px-Pocahontas-saves-Smith-NE-Chromo-1870.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Pocahontas-saves-Smith-NE-Chromo-1870.jpeg/580px-Pocahontas-saves-Smith-NE-Chromo-1870.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="1058" data-file-height="790" /></a>

In this <a href="/wiki/Chromolithography" title="Chromolithography">chromolithograph</a> credited to the New England Chromo. Lith. Company, ca. 1870, Pocahontas saves the life of John Smith. The scene is idealized and relies on stereotypes of Native Americans rather than reliable information about the particulars of this historical moment. There are no mountains in Tidewater Virginia, for example, and the Powhatans lived not in <a href="/wiki/Tipi" title="Tipi">tipis</a> but in thatched houses. And the scene that Smith famously described in his <i><a href="/wiki/The_Generall_Historie_of_Virginia,_New-England,_and_the_Summer_Isles" title="The Generall Historie of Virginia, New-England, and the Summer Isles">Generall Historie</a></i> (1624) did not take place outdoors but in a <a href="/wiki/Longhouse" title="Longhouse">longhouse</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Pocahontas is most famously linked to the English colonist <a href="/wiki/Captain_John_Smith" title="Captain John Smith" class="mw-redirect">Captain John Smith</a>, who arrived in Virginia with just more than a hundred other settlers in April 1607. After building a fort on a marshy peninsula poking out into the <a href="/wiki/James_River" title="James River">James River</a>, the Englishmen had numerous encounters over the next several months with the Natives of Tsenacommacah, some of them friendly, some hostile. Then, in December 1607, while exploring on the <a href="/wiki/Chickahominy_River" title="Chickahominy River">Chickahominy River</a>, Smith was captured by a hunting party led by Powhatan's younger brother (or close relative) <a href="/wiki/Opechancanough" title="Opechancanough" class="mw-redirect">Opechancanough</a> and brought to Powhatan's capital at <a href="/wiki/Werowocomoco" title="Werowocomoco">Werowocomoco</a>. In his 1608 account, Smith described a large feast followed by a long talk with Powhatan. He does not mention Pocahontas in relation to his capture; in fact, in this account, he does not meet Pocahontas for the first time until a few months later.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1616, however, Smith wrote a letter to <a href="/wiki/Anne_of_Denmark" title="Anne of Denmark">Queen Anne</a> in anticipation of Pocahontas's visit to England. In this new account, his capture included the threat of his own death: "... at the minute of my execution", he wrote, "she [Pocahontas] hazarded the beating out of her own brains to save mine; and not only that, but so prevailed with her father, that I was safely conducted to Jamestown."<sup id="cite_ref-Letter_to_Queen_Anne_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Letter_to_Queen_Anne-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Early histories did establish that Pocahontas befriended Smith and the Jamestown colony. Pocahontas often went to the settlement and played games with the boys there.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> When the colonists were starving, "every once in four or five days, Pocahontas with her attendants brought him [Smith] so much provision that saved many of their lives that else for all this had starved with hunger."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> As the colonists expanded their settlement further, the Powhatan felt their lands were threatened, and conflicts arose again.</p>
<p>In late 1609, an injury from a gunpowder explosion forced Smith to return to England for medical care. The English told the Powhatans that Smith was dead. Pocahontas believed that account and hence after, stopped visiting Jamestown. Much later, she learned that he was living in England when she traveled there as the wife of John Rolfe.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Historical records do not suggest that Smith and Pocahontas were lovers, or even that she was a suitable age for such a relationship. The romance is featured only (but repeatedly) in fictional versions of their relationship (such as the 1995 <a href="/wiki/Pocahontas_(1995_film)" title="Pocahontas (1995 film)">animated film by Walt Disney</a> and the 2005 Terence Malick film, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_World_(2005_film)" title="The New World (2005 film)">The New World</a></i>). The first romance was written about them in the early 19th century, suggesting the story's mythic appeal. Accounts of such a romance have been repeated in films made in the United States as late as 2009.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_views_on_Smith.27s_near_execution">Modern views on Smith's near execution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pocahontas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modern views on Smith's near execution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Eight years after his original account, in his <i>Generall Historie</i>, Smith expanded upon the story. Writing about himself in the third person, he explained that after he was captured and taken to the paramount chief, "two great stones were brought before Powhatan: then as many as could layd hands on him [Smith], dragged him to them, and thereon laid his head, and being ready with their clubs, to beate out his braines, Pocahontas the Kings dearest daughter, when no intreaty could prevaile, got his head in her armes, and laid her owne upon his to save him from death&#160;..."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The anthropologist Helen C. Rountree has argued that Smith's first "version of events [i.e., that he was interviewed by Powhatan] makes sense, given how eager the Indian leadership was to find out why the English had come and stayed in Virginia." Smith may have exaggerated or invented the account to enhance Pocahontas's standing. On the other hand, he may have been telling the truth. Some scholars have argued that the absence of the episode in Smith's earlier works should not be definitive evidence that it did not happen. Historian <a href="/wiki/J._A._Leo_Lemay" title="J. A. Leo Lemay" class="mw-redirect">J. A. Leo Lemay</a>, for instance, noted in his 1992 book that, as Smith's earlier writing was primarily concerned with geographical and ethnographic matters, he had no reason then to recount the story of Pocahontas.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Stan Birchfield has written that "Smith's writings are perfectly consistent with the truthfulness of the episode."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In <i>True Travels</i> (1630), Smith told a similar story of having been rescued by the intervention of a young girl after having been captured in 1602 by <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Turks</a> in Hungary. The historian <a href="/wiki/Karen_Ordahl_Kupperman" title="Karen Ordahl Kupperman">Karen Ordahl Kupperman</a> has suggested that he "presented those remembered events from decades earlier" when telling the story of Pocahontas.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> In other words, his story may have grown taller over time.</p>
<p>A different theory suggests that Smith may have misunderstood what had happened to him in Powhatan's <a href="/wiki/Longhouse" title="Longhouse">longhouse</a>. Rather than the near victim of an execution, he may have been subject to a tribal ritual intended to symbolize his death and rebirth as a member of the tribe.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kupperman_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kupperman-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> However, this theory is not well supported by evidence of Powhatan rituals or those of other North American Native groups.<sup id="cite_ref-Price243-4_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Price243-4-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> The historian Margaret Williamson Huber has argued that Powhatan, in this case, was being politically pragmatic by attempting to bring Smith, and so the English, into his chiefdom. According to Huber, Powhatan attempted to offer Smith rule of the town of Capahosic, which was close to Powhatan's capital at Werowocomoco. In this way, the paramount chief hoped to keep Smith and his men "nearby and better under control".<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Capture">Capture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pocahontas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Capture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Abduction_of_Pocahontas.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/The_Abduction_of_Pocahontas.jpg/220px-The_Abduction_of_Pocahontas.jpg" width="220" height="187" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/The_Abduction_of_Pocahontas.jpg/330px-The_Abduction_of_Pocahontas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/The_Abduction_of_Pocahontas.jpg/440px-The_Abduction_of_Pocahontas.jpg 2x" data-file-width="631" data-file-height="536" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Abduction_of_Pocahontas.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
In his engraving <i>The abduction of Pocahontas</i> (1619), <a href="/wiki/Theodor_de_Bry" title="Theodor de Bry">Johann Theodor de Bry</a> depicts a full narrative. Starting in the lower left, Pocahontas (center) is deceived by the <i><a href="/wiki/Weroance" title="Weroance">weroance</a></i> Iopassus, who holds as bait a copper kettle, and his wife, who pretends to cry. At center right, Pocahontas is put on the boat and feasted. In the background, the action moves from the Potomac to the York River, where negotiations for a hostage trade fail and the English attack and burn a Native American village.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Pocahontas' capture occurred in the context of the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Powhatan_Wars" title="Anglo-Powhatan Wars">First Anglo-Powhatan War</a>, a conflict between the Jamestown settlers and the Native Americans that began late in the summer of 1609.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> In the first years of war, the English took control of the <a href="/wiki/James_River" title="James River">James River</a>, both at its mouth and at the falls. Captain <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Argall" title="Samuel Argall">Samuel Argall</a>, in the meantime, pursued contacts with Native American groups in the northern portion of Powhatan's paramount chiefdom. The <a href="/wiki/Patawomeck" title="Patawomeck">Patawomecks</a>, who lived on the <a href="/wiki/Potomac_River" title="Potomac River">Potomac River</a>, were not always loyal to Powhatan, and living with them was a young English interpreter named <a href="/wiki/Henry_Spelman_of_Jamestown" title="Henry Spelman of Jamestown">Henry Spelman</a>. In March 1613, Argall learned that Pocahontas was visiting the Patawomeck village of Passapatanzy and living under the protection of the weroance Iopassus (also known as Japazaws).</p>
<p><sup id="cite_ref-RountreeHelen_a_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RountreeHelen_a-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>With Spelman's help translating, Argall pressured Iopassus to assist in Pocahontas's capture by promising an alliance with the English against the Powhatans.<sup id="cite_ref-RountreeHelen_a_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RountreeHelen_a-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> They tricked Pocahontas into boarding Argall's ship and held her for ransom, demanding the release of English prisoners held by her father, along with various stolen weapons and tools.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> Powhatan returned the prisoners, but failed to satisfy the colonists with the number of weapons and tools he returned. A long standoff ensued, during which the English kept Pocahontas captive.</p>
<p>During the year-long wait, she was held at <a href="/wiki/Henricus" title="Henricus">Henricus</a>, in modern-day <a href="/wiki/Chesterfield_County,_Virginia" title="Chesterfield County, Virginia">Chesterfield County, Virginia</a>. Little is known about her life there, although colonist Ralph Hamor wrote that she received "extraordinary courteous usage".<sup id="cite_ref-Hamor804_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hamor804-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> Linwood "Little Bear" Custalow, in a 2007 book, asserted that Pocahontas was raped during this time, citing oral tradition handed down over four centuries. According to Helen Rountree, "Other historians have disputed that such oral tradition survived and instead argue that any mistreatment of Pocahontas would have gone against the interests of the English in their negotiations with Powhatan."<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>At this time, the minister at Henricus, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Whitaker" title="Alexander Whitaker">Alexander Whitaker</a>, taught Pocahontas about <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> and helped her to improve her English. Upon her <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptism</a>, Pocahontas took the <a href="/wiki/Christian_name" title="Christian name">Christian name</a> "Rebecca".<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In March 1614, the standoff built up to a violent confrontation between hundreds of English and Powhatan men on the <a href="/wiki/Pamunkey_River" title="Pamunkey River">Pamunkey River</a>. At Powhatan's capital of Matchcot, the English encountered a group of some senior Native American leaders (but not Powhatan himself, who was away). The English permitted Pocahontas to talk to her countrymen. Pocahontas reportedly rebuked her father for valuing her "less than old swords, pieces, or axes", and told the Powhatan she preferred to live with the English.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_first_marriage">Possible first marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pocahontas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Possible first marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Current Mattaponi tradition holds that Pocahontas's first husband was Kocoum, brother of the Patawomeck <i>weroance</i> Japazaws, and that Kocoum was killed by the English after her capture in 1613.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> Today's Patawomecks believe that Pocahontas and Kocoum had a daughter, Ka-Okee, who was raised by the Patawomecks after her father's death and her mother's abduction.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>However, Kocoum's actual identity, location and even existence have been widely debated among scholars for centuries, with several historians<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (September 2014)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> arguing that the only mention of a "Kocoum" in any English document is taken from a brief statement written ca. 1616 by <a href="/wiki/William_Strachey" title="William Strachey">William Strachey</a> in England that Pocahontas had been living married to a "private captaine called Kocoum" for two years.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> Since 1614 is certainly when she married John Rolfe, and no other records even hint at any previous husband, it has accordingly been suggested that when Strachey wrote of the "private captaine called Kocoum" he was mistakenly referring to Rolfe himself, with the reference being later misunderstood as one of Powhatan's officers.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> There was a Powhatan military rank called <i>kokoraws</i>, sometimes translated 'captain', and scholarly debate has also raged<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Attribution_needed" title="Wikipedia:Attribution needed"><span title="This sentence may contain perspectives and opinions without stating whose they are. (September 2014)">attribution needed</span></a></i>]</sup> whether Strachey could have meant this as one of his famously divergent spellings, as a gloss to 'Captayne'. In addition, the date of Strachey's original statement has been widely disputed by numerous authors attempting either to make the case, or refute, that Pocahontas had been previously married. If there was such a marriage and Kocoum was not murdered, it likely ended, according to Powhatan custom, when Pocahontas was captured.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_to_John_Rolfe">Marriage to John Rolfe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pocahontas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Marriage to John Rolfe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Baptism_of_Pocahontas.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Baptism_of_Pocahontas.jpg/220px-Baptism_of_Pocahontas.jpg" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Baptism_of_Pocahontas.jpg/330px-Baptism_of_Pocahontas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Baptism_of_Pocahontas.jpg/440px-Baptism_of_Pocahontas.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="1982" /></a>

<p>During her stay in <a href="/wiki/Henricus" title="Henricus">Henricus</a>, Pocahontas met <a href="/wiki/John_Rolfe" title="John Rolfe">John Rolfe</a>. Rolfe's English-born wife, Sarah Hacker, and child, Bermuda Rolfe, died prior to his journey to Virginia. He had successfully cultivated a new strain of <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> there and spent much of his time tending to his crop. He was a pious man who agonized over the potential moral repercussions of marrying a heathen. In a long letter to the governor requesting permission to wed her, he expressed both his love for her and his belief he would be saving her soul claiming he was:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>motivated not by the unbridled desire of carnal affection, but for the good of this plantation, for the honor of our country, for the Glory of God, for my own salvation... namely Pocahontas, to whom my hearty and best thoughts are, and have been a long time so entangled, and enthralled in so intricate a labyrinth that I was even a-wearied to unwind myself thereout<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Pocahontas' feelings about Rolfe are unknown. She married him on April 5, 1614. Though frequently the wedding is placed at Jamestown, there is in fact no surviving record indicating where the ceremony took place. Possible sites include Henricus, Bermuda City, and Jamestown. <a href="/wiki/Richard_Buck_(chaplain)" title="Richard Buck (chaplain)">Richard Buck</a> presided. They lived for two years on Rolfe's plantation, <a href="/wiki/Varina_Farms" title="Varina Farms">Varina Farms</a>, which was located across the <a href="/wiki/James_River_(Virginia)" title="James River (Virginia)" class="mw-redirect">James River</a> from the new community of Henricus. Their son, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Rolfe" title="Thomas Rolfe">Thomas</a> was born on January 30, 1615.</p>
<p>Their marriage was not successful in winning the English captives back, but it did create a climate of peace between the Jamestown colonists and Powhatan's tribes for 8 years; in 1615, Ralph Hamor wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Since the wedding we have had friendly commerce and trade not only with Powhatan but also with his subjects round about us.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="England">England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pocahontas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sedgeford_portrait.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Sedgeford_portrait.jpg/170px-Sedgeford_portrait.jpg" width="170" height="264" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Sedgeford_portrait.jpg/255px-Sedgeford_portrait.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/01/Sedgeford_portrait.jpg 2x" data-file-width="330" data-file-height="513" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sedgeford_portrait.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The "Sedgeford Portrait", said to represent Pocahontas and her son, although its authenticity is debated.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Virginia Company of London had long seen one of its primary goals as the conversion of Native Americans to Christianity. With the conversion of Pocahontas and her marriage to an Englishman–all of which helped bring an end to the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Powhatan_Wars" title="Anglo-Powhatan Wars">First Anglo-Powhatan War</a>–the company saw an opportunity to promote investment. The company decided to bring Pocahontas to England as a symbol of the tamed New World "savage" and the success of the Jamestown settlement.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1616, the Rolfes traveled to England, arriving at the port of <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a> on June 12.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> They journeyed to London by coach, accompanied by a group of about eleven other Powhatans, including a holy man named <a href="/wiki/Tomocomo" title="Tomocomo">Tomocomo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> John Smith was living in London at the time and while Pocahontas was in Plymouth, she learned he was still alive.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith.2C_General_History.2C_page_261_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith.2C_General_History.2C_page_261-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> Smith did not meet Pocahontas, but wrote to Queen <a href="/wiki/Anne_of_Denmark" title="Anne of Denmark">Anne</a>, the wife of <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" title="James I of England" class="mw-redirect">King James</a>, urging that Pocahontas be treated with respect as a royal visitor. He suggested that if she were treated badly, her "present love to us and Christianity might turn to ... scorn and fury", and England might lose the chance to "rightly have a Kingdom by her means".<sup id="cite_ref-Letter_to_Queen_Anne_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Letter_to_Queen_Anne-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Pocahontas was entertained at various society gatherings. On January 5, 1617, she and Tomocomo were brought before the king at the old <a href="/wiki/Banqueting_House,_Whitehall" title="Banqueting House, Whitehall">Banqueting House</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Whitehall" title="Palace of Whitehall">Palace of Whitehall</a> at a performance of <a href="/wiki/Ben_Jonson" title="Ben Jonson">Ben Jonson</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Masque" title="Masque">masque</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Vision_of_Delight" title="The Vision of Delight">The Vision of Delight</a></i>. According to Smith, King James was so unprepossessing that neither Pocahontas nor Tomocomo realized whom they had met until it was explained to them afterward.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith.2C_General_History.2C_page_261_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith.2C_General_History.2C_page_261-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Although Pocahontas was not a princess in the context of Powhatan culture, the Virginia Company nevertheless presented her as a princess to the English public. The inscription on a 1616 engraving of Pocahontas, made for the company, reads: <small>"MATOAKA ALS REBECCA FILIA POTENTISS&#160;: PRINC&#160;: POWHATANI IMP:VIRGINIÆ"</small>, which means: "Matoaka, alias Rebecca, daughter of the most powerful prince of the Powhatan Empire of Virginia". Many English at this time recognized Powhatan to be the ruler of an empire, and they presumably accorded to his daughter what they considered appropriate status. Smith's letter to Queen Anne refers to "Powhatan their chief King".<sup id="cite_ref-Letter_to_Queen_Anne_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Letter_to_Queen_Anne-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Purchas" title="Samuel Purchas">Samuel Purchas</a> recalled meeting Pocahontas in London, writing that she impressed those she met because she "carried her selfe as the daughter of a king".<sup id="cite_ref-Purchas.2C_Hakluytus_Posthumus.2C_Vol_19.2C_p_118_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purchas.2C_Hakluytus_Posthumus.2C_Vol_19.2C_p_118-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> When he met her again in London, Smith referred to Pocahontas deferentially as a "Kings daughter".<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Pocahontas was apparently treated well in London. At the masque, her seats were described as "well placed",<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> and, according to Purchas, <a href="/wiki/John_King_(bishop)" title="John King (bishop)" class="mw-redirect">John King</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_London" title="Bishop of London">Bishop of London</a>, "entertained her with festival state and pomp beyond what I have seen in his greate hospitalitie afforded to other ladies."<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Not all the English were so impressed. According to Helen C. Rountree, "there is no contemporary evidence to suggest&#160;... that Pocahontas was regarded [in England] as anything like royalty." Rather, she was considered to be something of a curiosity and, according to one observer, she was merely "the Virginian woman".<sup id="cite_ref-RountreeHelen_b_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RountreeHelen_b-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Pocahontas and Rolfe lived in the suburb of <a href="/wiki/Brentford" title="Brentford">Brentford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">Middlesex</a>, for some time, as well as at Rolfe's family home at Heacham Hall, <a href="/wiki/Heacham" title="Heacham">Heacham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_England" title="Norfolk, England" class="mw-redirect">Norfolk</a>. In early 1617, Smith met the couple at a social gathering, and later wrote that when Pocahontas saw him, "without any words, she turned about, obscured her face, as not seeming well contented", and was left alone for two or three hours. Later, they spoke more; Smith's record of what she said to him is fragmentary and enigmatic. She reminded him of the "courtesies she had done", saying, "you did promise Powhatan what was yours would be his, and he the like to you." She then discomfited him by calling him "father", explaining Smith had called Powhatan "father" when a stranger in Virginia, "and by the same reason so must I do you". Smith did not accept this form of address because, he wrote, Pocahontas outranked him as "a King's daughter". Pocahontas then, "with a well-set countenance", said:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Were you not afraid to come into my father's country and caused fear in him and all his people (but me) and fear you here I should call you 'father'? I tell you then I will, and you shall call me child, and so I will be for ever and ever your countryman.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith.2C_General_History.2C_page_261_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith.2C_General_History.2C_page_261-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Finally, Pocahontas told Smith that she and her fellow Native Americans had thought him dead, but her father had told Tomocomo to seek him "because your countrymen will lie much".<sup id="cite_ref-Smith.2C_General_History.2C_page_261_56-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith.2C_General_History.2C_page_261-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pocahontas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:158px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pocahontas_gravesend.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Pocahontas_gravesend.jpg/156px-Pocahontas_gravesend.jpg" width="156" height="208" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Pocahontas_gravesend.jpg/234px-Pocahontas_gravesend.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Pocahontas_gravesend.jpg/312px-Pocahontas_gravesend.jpg 2x" data-file-width="480" data-file-height="640" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pocahontas_gravesend.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Statue of Pocahontas in <a href="/wiki/St_George%27s_Church,_Gravesend" title="St George's Church, Gravesend">Saint George's church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gravesend" title="Gravesend">Gravesend</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>, England</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In March 1617, Rolfe and Pocahontas boarded a ship to return to Virginia; the ship had only sailed as far as <a href="/wiki/Gravesend,_Kent" title="Gravesend, Kent" class="mw-redirect">Gravesend</a> on the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">river Thames</a> when Pocahontas became gravely ill.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> She was taken ashore and died in John Rolfe's arms at the approximate age of twenty-two. It is not known what caused her death, but theories range from <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>, to her having been poisoned.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> According to Rolfe, she died saying, "all must die, but tis enough that her child liveth".<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> Her funeral took place on March 21, 1617, in the parish of <a href="/wiki/St_George%27s_Church,_Gravesend" title="St George's Church, Gravesend">Saint George's</a>, Gravesend.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> The site of her grave is thought to be underneath the church's chancel, though since that church was destroyed in a fire in 1727 her exact gravesite is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> Her memory is honored with a life-size bronze statue at St. George's Church by <a href="/wiki/William_Ordway_Partridge" title="William Ordway Partridge">William Ordway Partridge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Descendants_and_legacy">Descendants and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pocahontas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Descendants and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Pocahontas and Rolfe had one child, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Rolfe" title="Thomas Rolfe">Thomas Rolfe</a>, who was born in 1615 before his parents left for England. Through this son, Pocahontas has many living descendants.</p>
<p>Two of Pocahontas's descendants have become <a href="/wiki/First_Lady_of_the_United_States" title="First Lady of the United States">First Lady of the United States</a>, both First Lady <a href="/wiki/Edith_Wilson" title="Edith Wilson">Edith Wilson</a> and First Lady <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Reagan" title="Nancy Reagan">Nancy Reagan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:158px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Edith_Wilson.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Edith_Wilson.jpg/156px-Edith_Wilson.jpg" width="156" height="197" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Edith_Wilson.jpg/234px-Edith_Wilson.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Edith_Wilson.jpg/312px-Edith_Wilson.jpg 2x" data-file-width="476" data-file-height="601" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Edith_Wilson.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Born and raised in Virginia, First Lady Edith Wilson was a descendant of Pocahontas.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Descendants of many <a href="/wiki/First_Families_of_Virginia" title="First Families of Virginia">First Families of Virginia</a> trace their roots to Pocahontas and Chief Powhatan, including such notable individuals as <a href="/wiki/Edith_Bolling_Galt_Wilson" title="Edith Bolling Galt Wilson" class="mw-redirect">Edith Bolling Galt Wilson</a>, wife of <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_W._Randolph" title="George W. Randolph">George Wythe Randolph</a>; Admiral <a href="/wiki/Richard_E._Byrd" title="Richard E. Byrd">Richard Byrd</a>; Virginia Governor <a href="/wiki/Harry_F._Byrd" title="Harry F. Byrd">Harry F. Byrd</a>; fashion-designer and <a href="/wiki/Socialite" title="Socialite">socialite</a> <a href="/wiki/Pauline_de_Rothschild" title="Pauline de Rothschild">Pauline de Rothschild</a>; actor <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Strange" title="Glenn Strange">Glenn Strange</a>; and astronomer and mathematician <a href="/wiki/Percival_Lowell" title="Percival Lowell">Percival Lowell</a>, who helped discover <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a>.</p>
<p>Her "blood" was introduced to the <a href="/wiki/Randolph_family_of_Virginia" title="Randolph family of Virginia">Randolph family of Virginia</a> via the marriage of her great-great-granddaughter, Jane Bolling, to <a href="/wiki/Richard_Randolph" title="Richard Randolph">Richard Randolph</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tilton_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tilton-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pamunkey" title="Pamunkey">Pamunkey</a> tribe of Virginia counts Pocahontas among its members.<sup id="cite_ref-pamunkey_npr_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pamunkey_npr-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_legend">Popular legend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pocahontas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Popular legend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pocahontas_1883.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Pocahontas_1883.jpg/170px-Pocahontas_1883.jpg" width="170" height="256" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Pocahontas_1883.jpg/255px-Pocahontas_1883.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Pocahontas_1883.jpg/340px-Pocahontas_1883.jpg 2x" data-file-width="403" data-file-height="608" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pocahontas_1883.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A 19th-century depiction</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After her death, increasingly fanciful and romanticized representations of Pocahontas were produced. The only contemporary portrait of Pocahontas is <a href="/wiki/Simon_van_de_Passe" title="Simon van de Passe" class="mw-redirect">Simon van de Passe</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Engraving" title="Engraving">engraving</a> of 1616. In this portrait, he tried to portray her Virginia- Native American features. Later portraits often portrayed her as more European in appearance.</p>
<p>The myths that arose around Pocahontas's story portrayed her as one who demonstrated the potential of Native Americans to be assimilated into European society. For example, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">United States Capitol</a> displays an 1840 painting by <a href="/wiki/John_Gadsby_Chapman" title="John Gadsby Chapman">John Gadsby Chapman</a>, <i>The Baptism of Pocahontas</i>, in the Rotunda. A government pamphlet, entitled <i>The Picture of the Baptism of Pocahontas</i>, explained the characters in the painting, and praised the Jamestown settlers for introducing Christianity to the "heathen savages".</p>
<p>In another development, Pocahontas's story was romanticized. Some writers preferred accounts of a love story between her and John Smith. The first to publish such a story at length was John Davis in his <i>Travels in the United States of America</i> (1803).<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> Perhaps the first surviving stage dramatization of the Pocahontas story was <a href="/wiki/James_Nelson_Barker" title="James Nelson Barker">James Nelson Barker</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Indian_Princess_(play)" title="The Indian Princess (play)"><i>The Indian Princess; or, La Belle Sauvage</i></a>. In the 19th century, <a href="/wiki/John_Brougham" title="John Brougham">John Brougham</a> produced a <a href="/wiki/Burlesque" title="Burlesque">burlesque</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Po-ca-hon-tas,_or_The_Gentle_Savage" title="Po-ca-hon-tas, or The Gentle Savage">Po-ca-hon-tas, or The Gentle Savage</a></i>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a href="/wiki/File:00OPocahontas.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/00OPocahontas.jpg/210px-00OPocahontas.jpg" width="210" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/00OPocahontas.jpg/315px-00OPocahontas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/00OPocahontas.jpg/420px-00OPocahontas.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="588" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:00OPocahontas.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<center>Pocahontas, reissue of 1907</center>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Jamestown Exposition celebrated the 300th anniversary of the Jamestown settlement in 1607 as the first permanent British colony in America. It was held in Norfolk from April 26 to December 1, 1907. Three stamps were issued; the 5-cent portrays Pocahontas, a friend to the colony, noted for saving John Smith's life. Her engraved portrait is modeled from Simon Van de Passe in 1616 which appeared in a book published by John Smith in 1624. The 5-cent paid large weight and foreign destination rates; about 8 million were issued.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Several <a href="/wiki/Pocahontas_(film)_(disambiguation)" title="Pocahontas (film) (disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect">films about Pocahontas</a> have been made, beginning with a silent film in 1924. <i><a href="/wiki/Captain_John_Smith_and_Pocahontas" title="Captain John Smith and Pocahontas">Captain John Smith and Pocahontas</a></i> was released in 1953 with <a href="/wiki/Jody_Lawrance" title="Jody Lawrance">Jody Lawrance</a> as the title role heroine. <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">The Walt Disney Company</a>'s 1995 animated feature <i><a href="/wiki/Pocahontas_(1995_film)" title="Pocahontas (1995 film)">Pocahontas</a></i> presented a fictional love affair between Pocahontas and John Smith. In addition, Pocahontas teaches Smith respect for nature. The main plot of James Cameron's 2009 film <i><a href="/wiki/Avatar_(2009_film)" title="Avatar (2009 film)">Avatar</a></i> is closely based on <i><a href="/wiki/Pocahontas_(1995_film)" title="Pocahontas (1995 film)">Pocahontas</a></i>. The sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/Pocahontas_II:_Journey_to_a_New_World" title="Pocahontas II: Journey to a New World">Pocahontas II: Journey to a New World</a></i>, depicts her journey to England and her meeting and falling in love with John Rolfe. In 2005 <a href="/wiki/Terrence_Malick" title="Terrence Malick">Terrence Malick</a> directed <a href="/wiki/The_New_World_(2005_film)" title="The New World (2005 film)"><i>The New World</i></a>,<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> a movie depicting the founding of the <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_Settlement" title="Jamestown Settlement">Jamestown</a>, Virginia settlement and depicts the role that Pocahontas had on the colony and its people, the movie featured <a href="/wiki/Q%27orianka_Kilcher" title="Q'orianka Kilcher">Q'orianka Kilcher</a> as Pocahontas. <i><a href="/wiki/Pocahontas:_The_Legend" title="Pocahontas: The Legend">Pocahontas: The Legend</a></i> is the second feature film based on her life.</p>
<p>There was a female northwestern Native American character in the 1939 film <i>In Old Montana</i> who was named after this historical princess. <a href="/wiki/Neil_Young" title="Neil Young">Neil Young</a> recorded <a href="/wiki/Pocahontas_(Neil_Young_song)" title="Pocahontas (Neil Young song)">a song about Pocahontas</a> on his album <i><a href="/wiki/Rust_Never_Sleeps" title="Rust Never Sleeps">Rust Never Sleeps</a></i> (1979).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Namesakes">Namesakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pocahontas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Namesakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Matoaka Whittle Sims, born 1844, <a href="/wiki/Pittsylvania_County,_Virginia" title="Pittsylvania County, Virginia">Pittsylvania County, Virginia</a>, descended on both sides from namesake Pocahontas</div>

<p>Numerous places and landmarks were named after Pocahontas:</p>
<ul>
<li>Pocahontas was the namesake for one of the richest seams of <a href="/wiki/Bituminous_coal" title="Bituminous coal">bituminous coal</a> found in Virginia and <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, and the Pocahontas Land Company, a subsidiary of the <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_and_Western_Railway" title="Norfolk and Western Railway">Norfolk and Western Railway</a>.</li>
<li>From 1930 into the 1960s, one of the <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_and_Western_Railway" title="Norfolk and Western Railway">Norfolk and Western Railway</a>'s named luxury trains was the "Pocahontas".</li>
<li>The town of <a href="/wiki/Pocahontas,_Virginia" title="Pocahontas, Virginia">Pocahontas, Virginia</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pocahontas_County,_West_Virginia" title="Pocahontas County, West Virginia">Pocahontas County, West Virginia</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Matoaca,_Virginia" title="Matoaca, Virginia">Matoaca, Virginia</a> is located in <a href="/wiki/Chesterfield_County,_Virginia" title="Chesterfield County, Virginia">Chesterfield County</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Appomattox_River" title="Appomattox River">Appomattox River</a>. County historians say this is the site of the Native American village <i>Matoax</i>, where she was raised.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Matoaka,_West_Virginia" title="Matoaka, West Virginia">Matoaka, West Virginia</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Amonate,_Virginia" title="Amonate, Virginia">Amonate, Virginia</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pocahontas,_Iowa" title="Pocahontas, Iowa">Pocahontas, Iowa</a> is in <a href="/wiki/Pocahontas_County,_Iowa" title="Pocahontas County, Iowa">Pocahontas County</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pocahontas,_Arkansas" title="Pocahontas, Arkansas">Pocahontas, Arkansas</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pocahontas,_Illinois" title="Pocahontas, Illinois">Pocahontas, Illinois</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fort_Pocahontas" title="Fort Pocahontas">Fort Pocahontas</a>, an <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> fortification in <a href="/wiki/Charles_City_County,_Virginia" title="Charles City County, Virginia">Charles City County, Virginia</a>.</li>
<li>Lake Matoaka, part of the campus of the <a href="/wiki/College_of_William_and_Mary" title="College of William and Mary" class="mw-redirect">College of William and Mary</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pocahontas_State_Park" title="Pocahontas State Park">Pocahontas State Park</a>, Chesterfield, Virginia.</li>
<li>MV <i>Pocahontas</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Lower_Thames_and_Medway_Passenger_Boat_Company" title="Lower Thames and Medway Passenger Boat Company">river tour boat</a> operated from Gravesend in London, UK.</li>
<li>Four <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> ships named <a href="/wiki/USS_Pocahontas" title="USS Pocahontas">USS&#160;<i>Pocahontas</i></a> and one named <a href="/wiki/USS_Princess_Matoika_(ID-2290)" title="USS Princess Matoika (ID-2290)">USS&#160;<i>Princess Matoika</i></a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pocahontas,_Mississippi" title="Pocahontas, Mississippi">Pocahontas, Mississippi</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Henrico_County,_Virginia" title="Henrico County, Virginia">Henrico County, Virginia</a>, a middle school has been named after Pocahontas and John Rolfe.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Matoaca_High_School" title="Matoaca High School">Matoaca High School</a>, located in <a href="/wiki/Chesterfield_County,_Virginia" title="Chesterfield County, Virginia">Chesterfield County, Virginia</a>. Their teams are called The Warriors.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pocahontas,_Alberta" title="Pocahontas, Alberta">Pocahontas, Alberta</a>.</li>
<li>Pocahontas Park, Vero Beach. Fl.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
